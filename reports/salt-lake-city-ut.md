# Salt Lake City, UT — Relocation Report

*Salt Lake County · Generated 2026-07-24*

Salt Lake City, UT presents a compelling but hazard-intensive relocation destination marked by significant natural disaster risks alongside strong civic infrastructure and favorable solar potential. While the region suffers from extreme earthquake risk (100th percentile), elevated wildfire and flood exposure, and persistent air quality challenges (85 AQI with 32 smoke days annually), it compensates with robust educational and healthcare systems, permissionless firearm carry and strong self-defense protections appealing to Second Amendment advocates, and an 85 solar score with reasonable electricity costs at 12.5¢/kWh. However, prospective residents must contend with steep housing costs (median $645k), high crime rates (864.2 violent crimes per 100k), acute drought stress requiring water-conservation mindset despite emerging solutions like Utah Lake System expansion, and a moderate tax burden (12.9% combined income/sales tax). The city's zone 7b climate offers a respectable 249-day growing season and mid-80s summers but delivers below-average sunshine (230 days) and minimal rainfall, while convenient airport access (30 minutes) and excellent grocery options provide lifestyle amenities. Overall, Salt Lake City suits relocators prioritizing strong schools and healthcare access, renewable energy potential, and Second Amendment freedoms who can absorb or mitigate substantial natural hazard risks and afford the premium housing market in an increasingly water-stressed region.

**Pros:** No active volcanoes nearby, Very low hurricane risk, Major metro area access
**Cons:** Fault line 0.5 miles away, 100th percentile earthquake risk, 98th percentile wildfire risk, 93rd percentile flood risk, Poor air quality (AQI 85), 32 smoke days/year

## Water Supply & Security

*This area faces high drought risk with Provo River allocations already slashed to 43.5% of normal demand due to low snowpack (76% of average), and groundwater is 1,500 feet deep, making wells challenging. However, new Utah Lake System capacity (3,100 acre-feet by 2026) and Aquifer Storage & Recovery systems (up to 5,900 acre-feet) are being developed to offset supply gaps.*

**Pros:** New Utah Lake System online 2026, ASR backup capacity 5,900 acre-feet, Diversified supply sources (surface + groundwater)
**Cons:** Drought risk high, snowpack only 76% normal, Provo River allocation cut to 43.5%, Groundwater 1,500 feet deep, Conservation mandates 15-25% reduction by 2035
