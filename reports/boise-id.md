# Boise, ID — Relocation Report

*Ada County · Generated 2026-07-24*

Boise, Idaho emerges as a compelling mid-sized relocation destination (population 238,853) offering exceptional strengths in climate, education, healthcare, and food infrastructure: a 244-day growing season with abundant sunshine (286 days/year), excellent public schools with nationally-recognized programs and permissive homeschooling regulations, and outstanding emergency healthcare anchored by St. Luke's Health System. The city also boasts low electricity rates, top-5 grid reliability, strong urban grocery access including legal raw dairy, and robust farmers markets operating nearly year-round. However, prospective relocators must carefully weigh significant headwinds: an acute and worsening water security crisis (exceptional drought conditions forcing Stage 2 conservation and a formal Drought Emergency Ordinance in 2026, despite 70% groundwater reliance and upstream dam backup), notable natural hazards including extreme summer wildfire risk in adjacent foothills, moderate earthquake and flood risks, and deteriorating solar economics due to closed net metering and 62% cumulative export credit reductions. Additional considerations include above-average crime rates (violent crime 222.5, property crime 1,335), limited commercial air connectivity (single hub; nearest major metros 6+ hours away), and car-dependent sprawl outside downtown despite strong transit-oriented core neighborhoods. The region's political bifurcation—Democratic urban core surrounded by Republican county and state—may influence local policy priorities and community fit, while moderate housing costs ($525k median) are partially offset by strict permitting and increasingly mandatory HOAs in newer subdivisions.

## Water Supply & Security

*Boise relies heavily on groundwater (70%) from the Boise aquifer via 80+ municipal wells, with surface water from Lucky Peak Reservoir providing backup. However, the region is currently experiencing exceptional drought—Ada County snowpack was near record lows in winter 2026, flows fell below 1,200 cfs on the Boise River, and the city declared Stage 2 water conservation two months early (April 2026) and a formal Drought Emergency Ordinance in July 2026, significantly eroding water security margins.*

**Pros:** Diverse backup systems (80+ wells, two treatment plants), Private wells legal up to 13,000 gal/day if permitted, Groundwater access still available in Ada County (not yet designated critical area)
**Cons:** Exceptional drought conditions, record-low snowpack, Stage 2 conservation declared early; Drought Emergency Ordinance in effect, Boise River flows 31% below April average; tight backup margins
