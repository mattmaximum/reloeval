## Water Supply & Security

- **Primary Water Source:** The City of Grand Junction's primary treated water supply is surface water from the Kannah Creek watershed on Grand Mesa (snowmelt/reservoirs feeding the Grand Junction and Kannah Creek water plants). Most of the rest of the Grand Valley/Mesa County is served by the Ute Water Conservancy District, whose primary raw-water sources are lakes, reservoirs and streams on the north side of Grand Mesa, with the Colorado River mainstem serving as a secondary/emergency source. ([source](https://www.gjcity.org/1389/City-Water-Conservation-Efforts), checked 2026-07-22)
- **Drought Risk:** High. Mesa County is currently in severe-to-exceptional drought; the winter of 2025-2026 produced the region's worst snowpack on record (roughly 25% of normal in the Colorado headwaters and about 18% in the Gunnison Basin), and Jan-May 2026 was the driest such period in 132 years of record. Colorado's Governor activated Phase 2 of the state's Drought Response Plan in March 2026, and Mesa County water providers have warned of possible watering restrictions. ([source](https://www.drought.gov/states/colorado/county/mesa), checked 2026-07-22) — *Drought risk is a time series, not a single score — reported as current category + historical frequency.*
- **Well Legality:** Private wells are legal but tightly regulated. Colorado groundwater is presumed tributary to surface streams and administered under the prior-appropriation ('first in time, first in right') doctrine. Mesa County lies outside any Designated Groundwater Basin, so new wells require a permit from the Colorado Division of Water Resources (State Engineer) under C.R.S. §37-90-137, and many uses require an adjudicated water right/augmentation plan. Small-capacity 'exempt' household wells (generally ≤15 gpm) can be permitted for indoor domestic use, but if a municipal or district water tap is reasonably available, state rules typically require obtaining permission from that provider before drilling a private well, and outdoor irrigation from such wells is often restricted or prohibited. ([source](https://dwr.colorado.gov/services/well-permitting), checked 2026-07-22)
- **Well Depth To Water Table (ft):** 20 ([source](https://www.energy.gov/sites/default/files/2022-09/GrandJunctionDandPFactSheet.pdf), checked 2026-07-22) — *Depth to water table varies parcel-to-parcel within a county — consult a local driller before relying on this figure.*
- **Water Rights Restrictions:** All water use in Colorado, including in Mesa County, is governed by the constitutional prior-appropriation doctrine ('first in time, first in right'): senior decreed water rights must be satisfied before junior rights when supply is short, so newer or undecreed users can be curtailed during dry years. The Colorado and Gunnison Rivers in the Grand Valley are heavily appropriated and subject to interstate Colorado River Compact obligations, making new water rights hard to secure; most groundwater withdrawals require a water court decree and, if they would injure a senior stream call, an approved augmentation plan. ([source](https://dwr.colorado.gov/services/water-administration/water-rights), checked 2026-07-22)
- **Backup Water Availability:** Backup supply exists but is constrained. Ute Water Conservancy District, which serves most of the Grand Valley, maintains the Colorado River mainstem as a secondary/emergency raw-water source in addition to its primary Grand Mesa reservoirs. The City of Grand Junction is studying conversion of Gunnison River gravel pits into new storage reservoirs to supplement its Kannah Creek watershed supply, which city officials project may need bolstering by around 2039; this project is still in the feasibility/study phase and not yet built. Given the record 2025-2026 drought and low mountain reservoir storage, backup capacity is currently limited, and the City has discussed drought-rate surcharges and watering restrictions if supplies tighten further. ([source](https://www.gjsentinel.com/news/western_colorado/grand-junction-looking-at-gunnison-river-to-supplement-water-supply/article_39baebe0-71dd-11ee-a29e-47ab96f949ad.html), checked 2026-07-22)

Open carry is generally legal statewide for those 18+ who can legally possess a firearm, though local municipalities may impose additional restrictions in 'sensitive' locations. - **Self Defense Legal Status:** Colorado is not a formal 'stand your ground' state by statute, but case law establishes no duty to retreat when using justified force in self-defense, both inside and outside the home. Colorado's 'Make My Day' law (Castle Doctrine, C.R.S. §18-1-704.5), enacted in 1985, allows occupants to use deadly force against an intruder who has unlawfully entered a dwelling if they reasonably believe the intruder intends to commit a crime and use physical force, without any duty to retreat. ([source](https://www.findlaw.com/state/colorado-law/colorado-self-defense-laws.html), checked 2026-07-22)
