# Carson City, NV — Relocation Report

*Carson City · Generated 2026-07-24*

Carson City, Nevada presents a mixed profile for potential relocators seeking an affordable, low-tax mountain community with some notable trade-offs. Situated at 4,737 feet elevation, this Republican-leaning city of 58,571 offers no state income tax, accessible median home prices ($507.5K), excellent healthcare infrastructure including a trauma center, and strong solar potential (88.0 score), supported by Nevada's constitutional carry laws and reportedly zero violent crime. However, prospective residents must carefully weigh significant drawbacks: high earthquake risk from a nearby fault line, elevated wildfire hazards (28 smoke days annually), limited sunshine (145 days/year) and a compressed growing season (138 days), plus concerning grid infrastructure challenges with 40% substandard substations and projected critical failures by 2030. Water security remains moderate with adequate backup systems through conjunctive use and county interconnections, though long-term capacity concerns loom post-2051. The city is highly car-dependent (walkability score 36) with minimal public transit and limited amenities, notably lacking major grocery chains, making it most suitable for remote workers or retirees comfortable driving for services and willing to accept natural hazard exposure in exchange for affordability and regulatory freedom.

## Water Supply & Security

*Carson City draws from three groundwater basins (29 wells average) plus surface waters from Ash Canyon Creek, Kings Canyon Creek, Carson River, and the Marlette/Hobart system. Drought risk is moderate—the Carson River watershed's 30-year plan warns some purveyor pumping may exceed capacity by 2051, with current streamflow at 78.7 cubic feet/second—but the city maintains high backup via conjunctive use strategy, 14 booster tanks, and cross-county interconnections to Carson Valley and Lyon County supplies.*

**Pros:** Conjunctive use maximizes surface water, reducing well reliance, Cross-county interconnections and 14 booster tanks provide strong backup, High groundwater availability with legal 30-well operation, Regional transmission mains to Carson Valley and Lyon County
**Cons:** Moderate drought risk; some purveyors may exceed pumping by 2051, Junior water rights holders face irrigation call restrictions during shortages, Deep well water table at 400 feet, Possible need for mitigation strategies and dedicated storage
