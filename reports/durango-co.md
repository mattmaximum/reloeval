# Durango, CO — Relocation Report

*La Plata County · Generated 2026-07-24*

Durango, Colorado presents a compelling mountain relocation destination for those seeking a small-town lifestyle (19,733 residents) with strong outdoor recreation, safety, and renewable energy potential, though it requires careful consideration of several constraints. The region offers good air quality, a robust economy with diverse amenities, reliable healthcare with on-site trauma care, and moderate climate with a 180-day growing season, alongside favorable Colorado self-defense laws and low violent crime (7.4 per capita). However, prospective residents must weigh significant challenges: severe water scarcity with only two weeks of emergency backup (constrained to 3,800 acre-feet yearly), high wildfire exposure affecting most properties in the Wildland Urban Interface with 18 annual smoke days, substantial housing costs ($940,000 median) combined with high taxes (13.8% combined income and sales), and below-average schools (6.2 rating). The area's limited mainstream retail options, low walkability (37), car-dependent layout, and dependence on aging infrastructure for critical resources like water and electricity mean Durango suits environmentally conscious, financially secure individuals prioritizing quality of life and outdoor access over convenience and affordability, but less so for those concerned about long-term water security or seeking diverse urban services.

Earthquake and hurricane risks are low, and you're safely distant from any fault lines (35 miles).*

**Pros:** Low hurricane risk, Good air quality (AQI 42), Low earthquake risk, Far from fault lines (35 mi)

## Water Supply & Security

*Durango depends on the Animas River and Lake Nighthorse for water, but faces high drought risk with aging infrastructure and a severely constrained allocation (3,800 ac-ft/year) shared primarily with tribal entities. Emergency backup is extremely tight—Terminal Reservoir holds only two weeks of supply, though Lake Nighthorse access would extend that to four months.*

**Pros:** Lake Nighthorse provides four-month buffer if accessible, Clear long-term water entitlements through ALP Project
**Cons:** High drought risk with aridification, Emergency reserves: only two weeks without Lake Nighthorse access, Limited allocation heavily committed to tribal entities, Aging water infrastructure
